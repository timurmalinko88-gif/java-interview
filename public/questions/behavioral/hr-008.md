# Salary Negotiation strategies
"What are your salary expectations?" (And how to handle the negotiation phase).

---ANSWER---

While not a STAR method question, this is one of the most critical parts of the HR interview. The goal is to maximize your compensation without alienating the recruiter. The general rule is: **Whoever gives a number first, loses**. Try to delay giving a specific number until you fully understand the role and they have decided they want to hire you.

However, many recruiters will push hard for a number early on to ensure you are within their budget.

### Examples (How to answer)
1. **Deflecting Early On**: "I'm very interested in this role and the technical challenges you described. Right now, I'm more focused on finding the right fit in terms of engineering culture and project scope. If we decide it's a mutual match, I'm sure we can come to an agreement on a competitive compensation package. Could you share the approved budget range for this position?"
2. **Giving a Range (If Forced)**: "Based on my research for a Senior Java Engineer role in this market, and considering my experience with high-load systems and cloud architecture, I am looking for a base salary in the range of $130,000 to $150,000. However, I consider the entire package, including equity, bonuses, and remote work flexibility, so I am open to discussing the details."
3. **Handling a Low Offer**: "Thank you so much for the offer. I'm very excited about the prospect of joining the team. The base salary is slightly lower than what I was anticipating based on my experience and current market rates. Is there any flexibility to bring the base up to $X? If we can agree on $X, I am ready to sign today."

### Red Flags (Life Analogy / Anti-patterns)
- **Giving a single, hard number too early**: "I want $140,000." If their budget is $160,000, you just lost $20k. If their budget is $120,000, they might reject you immediately without an interview.
- **Lying about your current salary**: Never lie. However, you are not obligated to share your current salary in many jurisdictions. Focus on your *expectations*, not your current pay.
- **Accepting the first offer immediately**: Always ask for 24-48 hours to review the written offer. There is almost always room for negotiation, even if it's just extra vacation days or a sign-on bonus.
- **Making it personal**: "I need more money because my rent increased." The company pays for the value you provide, not your personal expenses.

### Key Points
- Try to get the recruiter to state the budget range first.
- If you must give a number, give a carefully researched range, and state that the bottom of the range is your absolute minimum.
- Negotiate the *total compensation package* (bonus, equity, PTO, training budget), not just the base salary.
- Be polite, professional, and enthusiastic throughout the negotiation.
