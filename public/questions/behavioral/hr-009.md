# Questions the candidate MUST ask the interviewer
"Do you have any questions for us?"

---ANSWER---

Never say "No, you answered everything." Asking thoughtful questions at the end of the interview shows that you are engaged, analytical, and evaluating *them* just as much as they are evaluating *you*. It's a crucial part of the interview.

Tailor your questions to the person you are speaking with (HR, Tech Lead, Engineering Manager, etc.).

### Examples of Great Questions
1. **For the Engineering Manager / Tech Lead (Process & Culture)**:
   - "Can you describe the engineering culture here? How do you balance delivering new features with addressing technical debt?"
   - "What does the onboarding process look like for a new developer in the first 30/60/90 days?"
   - "How are technical decisions made within the team? Is it top-down from architecture, or is there room for developers to propose new tools/patterns?"
   - "How often does the team deploy to production, and what does the CI/CD pipeline look like?"
2. **For the Product Manager (Vision & Impact)**:
   - "What is the biggest challenge the engineering team is currently facing from a business perspective?"
   - "How do you define success for the person taking this role over the next 6 months?"
   - "Can you tell me about the roadmap for the next year? What features are you most excited about?"
3. **For HR / Recruiter (Company & Growth)**:
   - "What is the company's approach to professional development and training for engineers?"
   - "Why is this position open? Is it a new role due to growth, or a backfill?"
   - "What are the next steps in the interview process, and when can I expect to hear back?"

### Red Flags (Life Analogy / Anti-patterns)
- **Asking questions that are easily answered on the company website**: "What does your company do?" This shows you did zero preparation.
- **Asking only about perks and benefits early on**: "How much vacation do I get? Do you have free lunches?" Wait until the offer stage for these details, or ask HR, not the Tech Lead.
- **Asking overly aggressive questions**: "Why is your stock price dropping?" You can ask about company stability, but phrase it professionally.
- **Saying you have no questions**: This signals disinterest or lack of critical thinking.

### Key Points
- Prepare 3-5 questions before the interview.
- Ask questions that show you are thinking about how you can add value to their team.
- Use the opportunity to genuinely evaluate if the company is a good fit for *your* career goals.
