# Dealing with vague requirements and deadlines
"Tell me about a time you were given a task with vague requirements or an unrealistic deadline. How did you handle it?"

---ANSWER---

This question tests your **proactivity, product mindset, and time management**. The worst thing a developer can do is sit silently and wait for better requirements, or blindly build the wrong thing. Interviewers want to see that you take initiative to clarify ambiguity and negotiate scope when faced with strict deadlines.

Use the STAR method to show how you communicated with stakeholders to define the "What" before building the "How".

### Examples
1. **Vague Requirements**: "Situation: The product owner asked for a 'dashboard to track user activity', which was too vague to start developing. Task: I needed to define the exact metrics and data sources. Action: I didn't wait for a detailed spec. Instead, I scheduled a 30-minute sync with the PO and the marketing team. I brought a quick wireframe I drew on paper and asked, 'Is this what you mean?' We clarified that they only needed daily active users and top 5 API endpoints. Result: By defining the scope early, I built exactly what they needed in one sprint, saving us from weeks of back-and-forth revisions."
2. **Unrealistic Deadline (Scope Negotiation)**: "Situation: Marketing promised clients a new export feature by the end of the month, but my estimate showed it would take at least six weeks to build it properly with all requested formats (PDF, CSV, Excel). Task: Meet the deadline without burning out the team or delivering buggy code. Action: I met with the stakeholders and explained the technical constraints. I proposed an MVP approach: we would deliver the CSV export by the deadline (which covered 80% of client needs), and follow up with PDF and Excel in the next release. Result: The stakeholders agreed. We delivered the CSV feature on time, the clients were happy, and we didn't have to work weekends."
3. **Missing Dependencies**: "Situation: I was assigned a ticket to integrate a third-party payment gateway, but their API documentation was incomplete and their sandbox environment was down. Task: Keep the project moving despite external blockers. Action: I reached out to their support team immediately. While waiting, instead of doing nothing, I built our internal interfaces and mocked the expected API responses based on what little documentation existed. Result: When their sandbox finally came online a week later, our internal code was already written and tested. We completed the integration just one day behind the original schedule."

### Red Flags (Life Analogy / Anti-patterns)
- **Playing the victim**: "The PO is terrible at writing tickets, so I couldn't do my job." This shows a lack of ownership.
- **Building blindly**: "I just guessed what they wanted and built it." This wastes time and resources.
- **Agreeing to impossible deadlines**: "I just worked 80 hours a week to get it done." This shows poor negotiation skills and leads to burnout.

### Key Points
- Show that you are proactive in seeking clarification (meetings, wireframes, mockups).
- Demonstrate the ability to negotiate scope (MVP, phasing) rather than just pushing back deadlines.
- Highlight your ability to work on unblocked parts of a task while waiting for clarification on others.
