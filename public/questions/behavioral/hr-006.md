# Dealing with vague requirements and deadlines

## Framework
Demonstrate proactivity, communication skills, and agile thinking. Interviewers want to see that you don't just sit and wait for perfect instructions, but actively work to define the problem.

*   **Situation:** Describe a project where requirements were unclear or the deadline seemed impossible.
*   **Task:** Your objective was to gain clarity, manage expectations, and deliver value incrementally.
*   **Action:** Explain how you communicated with stakeholders, broke down the problem, and prioritized features.
*   **Result:** How you successfully delivered the project or renegotiated the scope/deadline successfully.

## Real-world Java Engineering Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The "Make it Faster" Request
*   **Situation:** The product owner asked me to "make the reporting module faster" before the end of the sprint, with no specific metrics provided.
*   **Task:** Define what "faster" means and establish a realistic goal.
*   **Action:** I set up a meeting with the PO to define SLAs. We agreed that a 3-second load time was acceptable. I then profiled the Java code, found a slow JPA query, optimized it, and added Redis caching for the most common reports.
*   **Result:** Load times dropped from 15 seconds to 2 seconds. The PO was thrilled, and we established a new process for defining performance metrics upfront.

### Scenario 2: The Moving Target Integration
*   **Situation:** We were integrating with a third-party payment API, but their documentation was incomplete and constantly changing.
*   **Task:** Deliver the integration on time despite the instability.
*   **Action:** I designed the system using the Strategy pattern in Java, isolating the third-party integration logic into discrete adapters. I also wrote mock servers to simulate their API behavior so my team could keep building the core business logic.
*   **Result:** When the third-party API finally stabilized, we only had to update one adapter class. We hit our deadline despite the external delays.

### Scenario 3: The Impossible Deadline
*   **Situation:** Management committed to a massive new feature launch for a conference in 3 weeks, which was normally a 2-month effort.
*   **Task:** Manage the unrealistic deadline without burning out the team or delivering broken software.
*   **Action:** I analyzed the requirements and proposed an MVP (Minimum Viable Product) approach. I negotiated with stakeholders to drop "nice-to-have" features (like complex async email notifications) and focus strictly on the core transaction flow.
*   **Result:** We successfully launched a stable MVP for the conference. The business was happy to show off the core functionality, and we scheduled the remaining features for the following sprints.

## Red Flags (What NOT to say)
*   **"I just guessed and built it."** Shows a lack of communication and risks wasting time building the wrong thing.
*   **"I worked 80 hours a week to meet the deadline."** Shows poor boundary setting and bad time management.
*   **Blaming the Product Owner/Business:** Complaining that "business never knows what they want" instead of taking responsibility for clarifying requirements.
*   **"I missed the deadline."** Without explaining how you communicated the risk early and tried to mitigate it.
