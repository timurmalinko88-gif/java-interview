# Resolving technical conflicts with a Tech Lead
"Tell me about a time you disagreed with your Tech Lead or a senior team member on a technical decision. How did you resolve it?"

---ANSWER---

This question evaluates your **communication skills, ego, and ability to handle conflict constructively**. The interviewer wants to know that you can advocate for your ideas professionally, use data over emotions, and ultimately commit to a team decision even if it wasn't yours.

Use the STAR method. The key is to show a collaborative approach rather than an adversarial one.

### Examples
1. **Architecture Disagreement**: "Situation: We were designing a new notification service. My Tech Lead wanted to use REST APIs for synchronous communication, but I believed an event-driven approach with Kafka would be more resilient. Task: I needed to convince the team without causing friction. Action: Instead of arguing in meetings, I built a quick Proof of Concept (PoC) comparing the latency and failure handling of both approaches under load. I presented the data objectively to the lead. Result: Seeing the metrics, the lead agreed that Kafka handled the spikes better. We adopted the event-driven approach, and it successfully scaled during our peak season."
2. **Library/Tooling Disagreement**: "Situation: The team was starting a new project. The lead wanted to use a heavy, legacy ORM framework they were familiar with, while I suggested using lighter Spring Data JDBC for performance and simplicity. Task: Resolve the disagreement and choose the best tool. Action: I scheduled a 1-on-1 to understand their concerns. They were worried about the learning curve. I offered to write the initial repository layer and document the patterns to make it easy for the team. Result: We compromised. We used Spring Data JDBC, and I ran a brief workshop for the team. The lead later acknowledged it made our queries much faster to write."
3. **Disagree and Commit**: "Situation: I proposed refactoring a large legacy module, but the Tech Lead rejected it, stating we didn't have the time before the upcoming release. Task: I felt strongly about the tech debt but needed to respect the timeline. Action: I explained my concerns about future bugs, but I listened to their perspective on the business constraints. I agreed to postpone the refactoring but asked if we could create a Jira ticket for the next sprint and add more test coverage in the meantime. Result: I 'disagreed and committed' to the release plan. We shipped on time, and in the next sprint, we tackled the refactoring safely."

### Red Flags (Life Analogy / Anti-patterns)
- **Saying you never disagree**: "I just do whatever the Tech Lead says." This shows a lack of initiative and critical thinking.
- **Being aggressive or stubborn**: "I argued until they gave up." This shows you are difficult to work with and prioritize being "right" over team harmony.
- **Going behind their back**: "I ignored them and wrote the code my way anyway." This destroys trust and is a massive red flag.
- **Taking it personally**: Framing it as an emotional battle rather than a technical discussion.

### Key Points
- Show that you use data, PoCs, and objective analysis to support your arguments, not just opinions.
- Demonstrate empathy: try to understand the *other person's* constraints (e.g., deadlines, familiarity).
- Highlight the "Disagree and Commit" principle: if the decision goes against you, you support it fully.
