# Resolving technical conflicts with a Tech Lead

## Framework
Show respect for authority while demonstrating technical maturity, logical reasoning, and the ability to compromise for the business's sake.

*   **Situation:** Describe a legitimate technical disagreement (e.g., architecture, tooling, approach).
*   **Task:** Your goal was to find the best solution for the project, not just to "win" the argument.
*   **Action:** How you presented data, facilitated discussion, and sought a middle ground.
*   **Result:** The chosen solution and the positive outcome on the project and the relationship.

## Real-world Java Engineering Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Microservices vs. Monolith
*   **Situation:** For a new internal tool, my Tech Lead wanted to build a complex microservices architecture, while I advocated for a simpler Spring Boot monolith to start.
*   **Task:** I needed to express my concerns about over-engineering and operational overhead without being confrontational.
*   **Action:** I created a brief document comparing the time-to-market and infrastructure costs of both approaches. I scheduled a 1-on-1 and presented the data, suggesting we build a modular monolith that could be easily split later if needed.
*   **Result:** The Tech Lead agreed with the data. We shipped the modular monolith a month early, and it established a strong trust between us.

### Scenario 2: Choosing a Database Technology
*   **Situation:** We were designing a new logging service. The Lead wanted to use a relational database (PostgreSQL), but I believed a NoSQL solution (Elasticsearch) was better suited for high-volume unstructured log data.
*   **Task:** Objectively evaluate both options.
*   **Action:** Instead of arguing, I built two quick proofs-of-concept (POCs) over the weekend and benchmarked the write speeds and query performance.
*   **Result:** The Elasticsearch POC handled 5x the volume. The Lead appreciated the data-driven approach and approved the NoSQL route. The service performed flawlessly in production.

### Scenario 3: Disagreement on Testing Strategy
*   **Situation:** My Lead insisted on achieving 100% code coverage, which was causing us to write brittle tests for simple getters and setters, slowing down feature delivery.
*   **Task:** Shift the focus from a vanity metric to meaningful testing.
*   **Action:** I brought examples of recent bugs that slipped through despite high coverage because we lacked integration tests. I proposed a compromise: 80% coverage on core business logic and mandatory integration tests for API endpoints.
*   **Result:** The Lead agreed to trial it for one sprint. Quality improved, and delivery speed increased, leading to a permanent change in our testing guidelines.

## Red Flags (What NOT to say)
*   **"I went behind their back."** Escalating immediately to a manager without trying to resolve it peer-to-peer.
*   **"I just gave up."** Shows a lack of conviction and passion for good engineering.
*   **Getting emotional/personal:** Framing it as a battle of egos rather than a technical discussion.
*   **Refusing to compromise:** Proving you cannot "disagree and commit" if the Lead ultimately makes a different call.
